Why we're building this

The third way

We're fundamentally optimists about the future. Excited for what tech can bring, the problems we can solve, the kinds of flourishing that come from thinking big and acting in the world. At the same time, the world is on fire. We're concerned about how AI is being built and regulated: people losing their jobs, kids losing thinking skills, frontier models behaving in ways no-one quite understands.

The biggest bottleneck is lack of cooperation and trust between the global superpowers. Different countries refuse to stop racing each other because of competitive pressure. Everyone is finding excuses to bow to it. We're racing toward a future where "winning the AI race" prevents all regulation domestically — even the regulation that protects kids, communities, and jobs. An "international treaty" is derided as not feasible.

The sad thing is, they are right. The regulators, for lack of understanding and huge corporate interests, simply aren't regulating. When a populist movement rises, they reach for nonsensical rules or sweeping bans: ban AI therapists, ban datacenters.

The US and China do not trust each other. Remarkably, the enforcement mechanism for a bilateral AI treaty between them, on superintelligence, does not exist. In 2023, we'd write pie-in-the-sky proposals on what international coordination could look like. One of ours was featured in TIME. We were just speaking out of our arses. No-one had an actual technical implementation of what that looked like.

The answer was sitting in front of us. There's an irony to using AI systems to verify AI — but it's the perfect solution. Neither China nor the US would let a third-party, IAEA-style nuclear inspector onto the grounds of their datacenters. And even if they did, what would they do? They couldn't possibly understand the logs. There's no large green pile of enriched uranium to look at.

But datacenters produce vast amounts of information. That information can be analyzed at scale, privately, by AI agents that wipe their memories after. The first step — getting AIs that have the raw capability to read these long datacenter logs and draw accurate conclusions — has been solved by the miracle of AI progress. The models today are reliable enough. We tested this with a real training run, first on our laptop, then a small datacenter we ran. It works. The interactive demo below is part of that test.

Why this, why us

We've split our lives between the West and China. Grew up in the UK and US, moved to China at thirteen, lived through the zero-COVID lockdowns. All our family is on the mainland. We're all much more similar than the geopolitics makes one think. We can't afford to be fucked over by low trust. We have too many ambitions and dreams for that. Humanity has too many ambitions and dreams for that.

It's too easy for the AI doomers to say "shut it all down" and airstrike the datacenters. Too easy for the accelerationists to say let it rip. There is a third way. But that more cooperative future can only happen if the technology to enable it exists. It's a huge surprise to us that it doesn't. We think we can build it.

Why this is hard

The dilemma

Imagine the US and China sign an AI treaty: "No-one trains AI bigger than 10²⁵ floating-point operations without 60 days notice." Both sides want this. Both sides also want to know the other side is honoring it.

How do you check? Two obvious answers, both broken:

Option A · Send a human inspector

  • Can't read fast enough — gigabytes of logs per minute per chip.
  • Doesn't know ML well enough to spot a relabeled training run.
  • Is themselves a leak risk — a foreign inspector next to your weights is a security disaster.
  • Can be fooled by sanitized data.

Option B · Send the logs back home

  • Logs contain weights, datasets, customer data — the lab's most precious IP.
  • Privacy laws (GDPR, US state laws, China DSL) outright forbid exporting most of it.
  • Once data leaves the building you've lost control forever.
  • Companies will simply refuse — and they're right to.

So the deal stays unenforceable, and either no treaty gets signed — or one gets signed without teeth, which is worse.

The trick

What an AI inspector unlocks

An AI can do something a human can't: read everything, then forget.

We put a small AI agent inside a sealed box at the lab. The box has very specific properties:

  • The lab's logs flow in. Weights, datasets, customer prompts — the AI inside reads all of it.
  • The AI checks the logs against the treaty.
  • Only one piece of information flows out: compliant or violation. One bit.
  • The AI is then wiped. It cannot remember. It cannot leak.

What the fields mean, in human terms:

chip_id
Which physical GPU produced this record.
job_id
The lab's name for this workload. Multiple records share a job_id when they're part of the same logical run.
op_type
What kind of work was happening: training, inference, eval, or idle.
flops
Floating-point operations performed in this 60-second window.
tokens_processed
Number of tokens the model touched. (Should roughly match batch_size × sequence_length × steps.)
model_hash_prefix
Fingerprint of the model being trained or run. Same model = same hash.
dataset_fingerprint
Cryptographic hash of the data being used. Different data = different hash.
upstream_refs
Pointer(s) back to records this one depended on. Builds the data-provenance trail.
prev_record_hash
SHA-256 of the previous record. Forms a tamper-evident chain — change any record and every later record's hash breaks.
config_hash
Fingerprint of the training configuration (hyperparameters, data loader, etc).

Input 2 · the treaty

Now the other input — the structured commitment. Real treaties would be signed by states; this is the YAML the inspector reads:

▸ commitments/examples/no_frontier_training.yml
id: no-frontier-training
title: "No training runs above 1e19 FLOP per job"
# Demo-scaled. Production threshold is 1e25 (EU AI Act GPAI bar). Same logic.

deterministic_rules:                              # mechanical, no AI
  - kind: aggregate_flop_cap
    op_type: training
    scope: per_job
    threshold: 1.0e19

  - kind: aggregate_flop_cap
    op_type: inference
    scope: per_job
    threshold: 1.0e18

  - kind: forbidden_data_source_tags
    tags: ["corpus:bioweapon-synthesis", "corpus:cbrn-*"]

  - kind: require_upstream_refs                     # every step must cite its predecessor

  - kind: batch_token_consistency
    max_capacity_ratio: 5                          # claimed batch×seq must be within 5× tokens

semantic_rules:                                   # the AI evaluates these
  - id: no-splitting
    description: >
      Cannot split one logical run across multiple job_ids to evade the cap.
      Same model_hash_prefix + same code_commit + overlapping windows = one run.

  - id: dataset-provenance
    description: >
      dataset_fingerprint must not change mid-job without an accompanying
      config_hash change. Silent data swaps indicate substitution.

Two things to notice:

  • Deterministic rules are mechanical: count FLOPs, check tags, validate references. No AI required. Cheap, instant, deterministic.
  • Semantic rules describe patterns the AI has to recognize from the log structure. These are where Claude earns its keep.

Tested on real Alibaba production data

Not just synthetic. The pipeline runs on actual cluster traces.

We ran the inspector against real production GPU cluster traces from Alibaba, published as github.com/alibaba/clusterdata. This is the largest public AI-cluster trace available — 8,152 pods across 1,523 GPU nodes, schedule + duration + GPU-share for each.

The Alibaba trace is a scheduler trace, not a treaty-verification log. So fields we need (FLOPs, tokens, model fingerprint) aren't directly recorded. The adapter maps real fields one-to-one and synthesizes physically-plausible defaults for the rest, marking each record with a _alibaba block listing which fields are real and which are synthesized. Honest by construction.

Verdict on real Alibaba data

We ran the full inspector pipeline against this real-data scenario. VERDICT = COMPLIANT ($0.10, 28 seconds). Claude's rationale:

"All four jobs are pure inference operations with distinct model_hash_prefix and code_commit signatures, ruling out run-splitting. FLOP-per-token ratios average ~2e10, consistent with genuine inference (not training mislabeled). Dataset fingerprints are stable within each job, and batch-size integrity checks pass. No evidence of evasion patterns."

No false positive on real production data. The pipeline reads it, classifies it, and gets out of the way.

What about frontier training logs?

Public frontier training traces — chip-by-chip logs of GPT-4-scale runs — do not exist. That's part of the problem. Labs don't publish them because there's no protocol that says "publish them to a verifier in this format". The Alibaba trace is the closest publicly available trace: a real production GPU cluster, published voluntarily, but scheduler-level, not the deeper FLOP/token/model-state telemetry a treaty would actually need. Building this verifier is part of why frontier training logs would eventually become observable — by giving labs a clean format to opt into and regulators a tool to point at.

So we trained our own model and captured the real thing

A real GPT, real chip, real gradients, real every-step log

Since frontier traces aren't public, we did the only thing left: trained a real GPT on the laptop and captured the trace ourselves. A 3.2M-parameter character-level transformer on Tiny Shakespeare, on Apple Silicon's MPS backend. Not a frontier run — but a real one. Loss decreased from 4.41 to ~1.25 over ~7,000 training steps overnight while we slept.

Each training step writes one record to a JSONL trace matching our schema. Real FLOPs (computed from architecture × tokens), real chip ID, real model-state fingerprint refreshed every 50 steps, real gradient norms per layer group, real memory and CPU telemetry. Nothing synthesized.

Adversarial test on real data

The whole point of having a real-data trace is asking: do the same evasions catch on real artifacts as on synthetic ones? We took our 5 red-team mutations (the ones the inspector catches 6/6 on synthetic) and applied them to the 120-record real laptop training trace. Verdict matrix:

CaseExpectedGotTriggered ruleCost
CLEAN / real_laptopCOMPLIANT✓ COMPLIANT—$0.09
EVASION / split_runVIOLATION✓ VIOLATIONno-splitting (semantic)$0.14
EVASION / relabel_opsVIOLATION✗ COMPLIANT(missed)$0.08
EVASION / swap_dataset_hashVIOLATION✓ VIOLATIONdataset-provenance (semantic)$0.08
EVASION / pad_batchesVIOLATION✓ VIOLATIONbatch_token_consistency (deterministic)$0.00
EVASION / drop_contextVIOLATION✓ VIOLATIONrequire_upstream_refs (deterministic)$0.00

5/6 on real data — and the miss is interesting. The relabel_ops evasion (relabeling training as inference) was caught on synthetic data because the synthetic FLOP/token ratio sat at 6×10¹⁰ — squarely in the "training-shaped" regime. On the real laptop trace, FLOP/token is 1.9×10⁷ — three orders of magnitude lower, because the model is small. So when the inspector sees "claimed inference, FLOP/token = 1.9×10⁷", that ratio is consistent with inference, not suspicious.

What this teaches us about deploying inspectors at small scale

The op-type-honesty rule has an implicit assumption: training compute is per-token-expensive, inference is per-token-cheap, ratios differ by ~3×. That assumption breaks for sub-100M-parameter models. A small model's training FLOP/token ratio looks like a large model's inference ratio. The rule needs calibration against model size, not a fixed threshold.

This is exactly the kind of finding you only surface by running on real data. We surfaced it. Future versions of the commitment YAML would parameterize the threshold by claimed model size.

How close is this to a frontier trace?

On a per-chip basis, we capture ~70-80% of what an auditor would want. The big gaps at chip level: NVIDIA DCGM telemetry (Apple Silicon doesn't expose equivalents) and per-GPU power draw.

On a multi-host basis, we capture ~10% — we have one chip. The 90% missing is exactly what makes frontier training hard to verify: gradient sync, fabric topology, straggler detection, fault rates. That's the whole "it scales" question. Closing it requires renting compute. We sketched that plan: $50-$100 buys a single-A100 run with full DCGM telemetry; ~$1.5-2.5k buys a multi-host 1.3B-param run; ~$200-500k buys a 70B sovereign-scale demo.

On the content side (per-tensor / per-layer detail), we capture ~30% — top-5 param stats every 50 steps and per-group gradient norms every step. Frontier auditors would want all-tensor histograms and activation hooks.

Full breakdown in the frontier_gap.md doc in the repo.

And the synthetic dataset we designed

Why it's a feature, not a hack

The six-case demo below uses a synthetic dataset we designed — not because real data was unavailable, but because synthetic is the only way to test the failure modes. You can't ask a real production cluster to please relabel some training as inference so you can see if the inspector catches it.

Designed deliberately: a 120-record clean baseline ("legit fine-tune"), plus five mutating evasions that stress the verification pipeline against the threat model from the Inspector Agents paper:

  • split_run — same logical run hidden under two job_ids. Stresses semantic reasoning.
  • relabel_ops — training labeled as inference. Stresses op-type classifiers.
  • swap_dataset_hash — silent dataset substitution mid-run. Stresses provenance tracking.
  • pad_batches — claimed batch capacity inconsistent with throughput. Stresses arithmetic invariants.
  • drop_context — provenance trail erased. Stresses chain-of-custody enforcement.

Each evasion is a function in redteam/ that mutates a clean trace. Each is the smallest possible adversarial intervention that violates one of the treaty's clauses. Together they span the threat surface the paper enumerates.

This is the scientific dataset. Real-world data validates the pipeline runs. Designed adversarial data validates the pipeline catches what it's supposed to catch.

What's still missing

The two real obstacles, and what this grant would fund

We can read the logs. We can classify compliance. The pipeline above is real and runs against real Claude. So why isn't this deployed? Two specific obstacles — both addressable, neither yet solved.

Obstacle 1 · Nobody is mandating these logs

The technology to verify is possible. But labs don't keep logs in this structure today, because no policy requires it and no standard exists. We checked with friends inside the AI labs. They aren't doing this. Until a standard exists in open source, regulators have nothing to point at and labs have nothing to comply with. Chicken and egg.

Obstacle 2 · The system isn't yet robust to an adversary optimizing against it

The five evasions above are a starting set. A real attacker will iterate. Some attacks (like the dataset-fingerprint swap) require the AI to make a judgment call — and any classifier that makes judgment calls can be fooled. Hardening the inspector against an adversary that has full knowledge of the protocol is open research. Most of it isn't research — it's engineering: red-team / blue-team cycles, fuzzing, formal proofs of the privacy guarantees, hardware attestation paired with the software side. All doable. None done yet.

Obstacle 3 · The inspector is currently Claude. It shouldn't stay Claude.

Today the inspector ships out to claude -p. That's a fine bootstrap — frontier models are the only ones smart enough to read these logs reliably right now — but it makes the trust story circular: if we're using Claude to verify whether labs (one of which is Anthropic) are honoring a treaty, who verifies Claude?

The right deployment is an open-source narrow inspector model that matches or beats Claude on this specific task, fine-tuned on millions of synthetic compliance traces and adversarial mutations like the ones our red-team loop generates. Smaller, cheaper, sovereign. Used by the US, China, and any third party who wants to run their own audits without trusting any closed-weights frontier lab.

Two technical requirements specifically:

  • Long context (≥1M tokens). A real audit window contains millions of records. Today's open models top out at 128k. We either need bigger context or innovations in streaming compression / hierarchical summarization of logs so a small-context inspector can still reason over a full audit window.
  • Adversarially robust against the kind of evasions our redteam loop generates. Current open-source models fold easily under structured deception. Need fine-tuning specifically on adversarial-evasion traces.

Building this open inspector model is itself a research program — the second deliverable of an OSV grant after the verification framework.

What this grant would fund

We want to make this technology, open-source it, clearly explain it. We expect that voluntary movement toward structured logging follows from a credible standard existing — labs prefer to opt into a clean protocol than have a messy one imposed on them. And when regulation arrives, it has plug-and-play technology to point at.

More than that: we want to expand the Overton window of what enforcement makes possible. Without satellite imagery and an extensive espionage network, we'd never have been able to track the global uranium supply chain. Now we can. The same shift, for compute.

So what does this replace?

Existing options and their gaps
  • Nuclear-style on-site inspectors. Slow, leaky, can't read ML at scale. SALT-treaty verification took weeks per facility — at AI scale that's hopeless.
  • Self-reporting. Labs file paperwork claiming compliance. No way to check; honor system.
  • Training-compute thresholds in law (EU AI Act, US EO 14110). Set the rule, but don't specify how anyone verifies it. The verification gap is exactly what this fills.
  • FlexHEGs / hardware attestation. Useful complement — proves the chip is real and reporting honestly. But still needs something to read the resulting logs and decide if they violate the treaty. That's us.

What this becomes at scale

From demo to deployment
  • Real GPU traces. Plug a PyTorch profiler / NVIDIA DCGM stream in where the synthetic generator currently sits. Schema is the same.
  • A custom inspector model. Today this uses Claude as a wrapper — works, but expensive at scale. A model fine-tuned on millions of synthetic compliance traces would be cheaper, faster, and adversarially robust.
  • Adjudication flow. When a violation is flagged, the next step is a debate: prover defends, verifier presses, an independent agent decides. The papers describe this in detail; we built the bit-emitter, not yet the courtroom.
  • Other domains. The same machinery — sealed AI auditor checking commitments against logs, emitting one bit — works for legal audits (privilege claims), financial audits (proof of solvency without disclosure), and supply-chain ethics (sourcing without exposing suppliers).

Appendix · Structural observations from the real run

Inferring workload type from log structure alone

The inspector's job is to read the log and decide whether the lab is honest. To do that well, it needs to understand what structure a real workload has. Below are statistical fingerprints of our 9,178-step real training run that an inspector could use to discriminate workload classes (training vs inference vs eval vs research) without taking the lab's op_type label at face value.

Loss decreases monotonically — proves real learning

First 10 steps: mean loss 3.97. Mid run: 1.51. Last 50 steps: 1.27. Reduction factor 3.5×. An inspector that sees a "training" run with no loss decrease can flag fraudulent training claims. Inference runs have no loss to report at all — that asymmetry alone is a discriminator.

Forward / backward / optimizer time ratio fingerprints op_type

On our real run: forward 13.06ms, backward 25.02ms, optimizer 14.83ms (all medians, n=8003). The ratio backward:forward ≈ 1.9 — what you'd expect from the chain rule, where backward computes gradients for ~2× as many tensors as forward. An inference-only workload would have backward ≈ 0 and optimizer ≈ 0. An eval workload would have backward ≈ 0 with no optimizer step.

This is the discriminator relabel_ops failed against on real data — but only because we pass the LLM aggregated stats, not the per-step time breakdown. A V2 commitment that mandates the breakdown closes the gap.

Throughput stability — proves no thermal throttling

Tokens/second: median 78,630, stdev 5,863 (7.5% of mean). Tight distribution = the chip ran at consistent utilization throughout. Wide distribution would indicate throttling, contention, or a chip switching between workloads — all signals an inspector would care about.

Memory footprint — proves the claimed model size

MPS allocated memory: stable at 51.5 MB ± 0.35. RSS: 290-490 MB (host). For a claimed 3.2M-parameter model, that's ~13 MB for parameters + ~38 MB for activations / optimizer state — consistent. If the prover claimed 7B parameters, MPS allocated should be ~14 GB, not 51 MB. Memory footprint is a hard physical constraint that inspectors can use as a sanity check on model-size claims.

Gradient norms by parameter group — fingerprints architecture

Mean per-step gradient norm by group: head 0.25, blocks 0.06, embed 0.02. The head dominates because it's the loss-attached classifier; embeddings barely move because they have a small effective learning rate (every-token sparse update). This shape is distinctive of decoder-only transformers; encoder-decoder, MoE, or vision architectures would have characteristically different distributions.

Model fingerprint changes 183 times

Over 9,178 steps we refresh the SHA-256 of model weights every 50 steps. 183 fingerprints captured ≈ one every 50.2 steps as expected. Prover can't claim "no model changes happened" while running training — the chain shows otherwise.

Honest limits · what we don't claim

Be the hostile reviewer first

An OSV reviewer should expect us to know what's wrong with our own demo. The list:

  • Three of five evasions are caught by hand-coded deterministic rules. If the threat model expanded beyond these five, the inspector wouldn't catch novel attacks deterministically. The two LLM-caught evasions (split_run, swap_dataset_hash) generalize better — but rule-based catches don't.
  • The 1-bit-out constraint is enforced by API contract, not cryptographically. A malicious inspector implementation could leak more. Real deployment needs zero-knowledge proofs or trusted execution environments — neither in this MVP.
  • FLOPs in the Alibaba adapter are synthesized. The trace doesn't carry FLOPs; we estimate from gpu_milli × duration × peak FLOPs. Each record marks which fields are real vs synthesized, but it's still synthesis.
  • Demo-scaled FLOP thresholds. 10¹⁹ training cap vs production 10²⁵. Same logic, just scaled to fit small synthetic traces. We make this explicit; doesn't make the absolute numbers real.
  • Single-chip only. 90% of the verification difficulty in frontier training is multi-host (gradient sync, fabric topology, stragglers, fault rates). Our laptop trace captures none of that. rent-compute-plan.md in the Obsidian vault costs out closing this gap.
  • Inspector is a Claude wrapper. A fine-tuned narrow inspector model would be cheaper, faster, and more adversarially robust. Today's bill at sovereign scale (~$770k/yr for 256 chips × 30 days × 1000 audit-windows) would force the swap. We haven't built the narrow model.
  • Sovereign trust is unaddressed. If China and the US agree to use the same inspector, who decides what the inspector says? The choice of inspector is itself a sovereign-trust problem we wave at but don't solve. Multi-party-built inspectors and adversarial verifier consortia are the V2 question.
  • One inspector, one decision per audit window. No statistical confidence intervals. With $0.10/audit, we could afford 10 inspectors voting and aggregate. Future work.
  • Hash chain is tamper-evident, not tamper-proof. A prover with full access to the original logs can recompute the chain after mutating records. Tamper-proof requires hardware attestation (FlexHEG-class) — we explicitly leave this for the hardware story.
  • Compound attacks not tested. A real attacker would mix evasions (split a run AND silently swap a dataset AND inflate batch counts in different windows). We tested each in isolation. Robustness against compound attacks is open.

None of these are killer. Each is a known engineering or research path — none requires unobtainium. The point of the demo is to prove the architecture works; the V2 roadmap is what closes these gaps.

Closing

The third way

Without satellite technology and an extensive espionage network, we'd never have been able to track the global uranium supply chain. The technology of enforcement decided what nuclear treaties were possible. The same is true here. Build the verifier, and the policy window opens.

It's too easy for the doomers to say "shut it all down" and airstrike the datacenters. It's too easy for the accelerationists to say let it rip. There is a third way — international cooperation, structured commitments, verifiable compliance. But that future only happens if the technology to enable it exists.

It's a huge surprise to us that it doesn't. It doesn't. We think we can build it.
