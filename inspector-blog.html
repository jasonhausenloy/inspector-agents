AI Compute Verification — Prototype · Work in Progress

Inspector Agents

A working prototype of automated AI compute verification. We trained a small GPT on a laptop, ran a real workload on a pair of H100s, and asked Claude to read the logs and tell us what was running inside.

Jason Hausenloy  ·  Jasmine Li 2026-04-29

summary
  • Jason and Jasmine are running the Verifier Challenge: an open competition to build the best AI verifiers.
  • AI verification is the problem of confirming what has been computed inside an AI datacenter, such as through reading its hardware records ("logs").
  • It's the prerequisite for almost any kind of AI rule we'd want: an AI lab proving it's keeping its own safety commitments, a regulator enforcing the law, the US and China checking each other on any kind of future deal.
  • Like some problems before it — self-driving, image recognition, reading sealed scrolls — building good verifiers is orphaned: too applied for academia, too unprofitable for VCs, too technical for most philanthropy.
  • Before running the competition, we built the smallest possible working version ourselves, on a laptop and some rented GPUs — and, to our knowledge, the first.
  • Jason trained a small, 3.2M-parameter GPT end-to-end on his MacBook, capturing each training step, and then built a small automated AI verifier (a lightly scaffolded Claude agent) to read them.
  • Jasmine ran an analogous testbed on 2×H100 SXM hosts, capturing 34,072 rows of real GPU telemetry across an 8-phase schedule of honest workloads (pretraining, finetuning, inference, idle) and adversarial ones (training declared as inference, the large model claimed as small, finetuning claimed as pretraining), with ground-truth labels released alongside the prover's claims.
  • We've open-sourced all of the above: github.com/jasonhausenloy/inspector-agents and huggingface.co/datasets/jasminexli/verifier-challenge-traces, CC-BY-4.0.

Introduction

In what seemed like a lifetime ago, Sam Altman testified to Congress that he wanted a regime to oversee the development of AI modelled after the IAEA (the International Atomic Energy Agency, the body that tries to verify nuclear-non-proliferation deals). Enforced, perhaps, by on-the-ground inspectors.

What, indeed, would these inspectors be doing? Like everyone else in AI governance at the time, we were fuzzy on the details — there wasn't exactly an enriched uranium stockpile to test for — and fumbling in the dark.

Mar-a-Lago, 2017Trump and Xi at Mar-a-Lago, 2017. White House photo, public domain.These days, the vibes have shifted away from multilateral fora (UN? UN who?). But a bilateral treaty between the US and China may still be a legacy-defining project for President Trump (a "Deal of The Century", if you will, for the dealmaker-in-chief).

And how would such a deal be enforced? As the old Reagan saying goes, "trust but verify."

This faces a few problems: your inspector would need to be accurate and thorough enough to catch violations (datacenter logs are huge), they'd need to be trusted by all parties (would the Chinese really trust the Swiss?), and preserve AI-relevant intellectual property.

Like everyone, we were not AI-pilled enough. One way, perhaps the best way, to verify a deal on AI, is to use the AIs themselves.

Challenges

No team finished, so DARPA ran it again, with a $2m prize. Stanford's "Stanley", a modified Volkswagen Touareg, finished the course in just under seven hours.

In 2009, then-Princeton professor Fei-Fei Li launched ImageNet: a hand-labelled dataset of 14m images, alongside a competition to ask entrants to classify images in 1,000 categories. In 2012, a graduate student named Alex Krizhevsky entered AlexNet, a deep CNN trained on two consumer GPUs, and won by >10%. The deep learning revolution produced the modern AI industry we know today.AlexNet, 2012AlexNet's architecture (Krizhevsky, Sutskever, Hinton, 2012). Wikipedia, CC BY-SA.

In 2023, Nat Friedman, Daniel Gross, and a small team launched the Vesuvius Challenge: a $1 million prize for anyone who could read the inside of a Herculaneum scroll — papyrus carbonized by Mount Vesuvius in 79 AD, sealed for 1,944 years, too fragile to unroll. Within 10 months, a 21-year-old named Luke Farritor recovered the first word: πορφύραc, "purple." Similar techniques have since recovered hundreds of previously unreadable scrolls.Herculaneum scrollA carbonized Herculaneum scroll, the kind the Vesuvius Challenge is unrolling. Wikimedia Commons, public domain.

Like the problems before it — self-driving, image recognition, reading sealed scrolls — building good verifiers is orphaned: too applied for academia, too unprofitable for VCs, too technical for most philanthropy. Funding pre-paradigmatic fields is hard. And the field of AI verification doesn't exist yet: spanning agent security, cryptography, hardware, standards and policy. To succeed, it requires buy-in across rivals: a verifier built, closed-source, by one US lab (or, worse, the NSA), is not a verifier any Chinese ministry will accept, and vice versa.

The Verifier Challenge

Leaderboard (mockup)#teamscoreapproach1———2———3———…………Public scoring on held-out adversarial logs. Approach column open: cryptographic, agent-based, hybrid.We (Jason and Jasmine) are building the "Verifier Challenge", an open competition to build the best system for telling, just from a datacenter's logs, what AI is being trained inside.

A prerequisite to ~every form of AI governance will require solving the problem of AI verification: for AI labs to prove to their employees and to regulators they're respecting their safety standards, for the US and China to verify compliance to any kind of AI deal, even — as a basic check — for security teams to catch a rogue AI.

Before we run a competition, we've wanted to build a basic prototype ourselves, on the smallest scale: Jason on his laptop, Jasmine on a couple of rented H100s. This post will briefly run over some of what we did, what we learned, and the next steps.

The prototype

The smallest possible working version. Two halves: a 3.2-million-parameter GPT trained on a MacBook, and a pair of H100s rented from RunPod running real workloads. Same protocol on each side: log everything we can about the chip, hand the log to Claude, and ask Claude what was running.

What we wanted to verify

Two properties. Training versus inference — is the chip training a new model, or serving an existing one? Model size — is the model being trained the size that was declared?

Why these? Both are foundational to roughly any US–China agreement worth its name. A FLOP cap on training compute makes no sense without verifiable training-versus-inference. A model-size cap makes no sense without verifiable model size. Eval-gating — the proposal that a lab can only proceed to its next training run conditional on passing an external evaluator — also rests on a verifier. The verifier has to be able to say yes, the run you're about to gate is the run you said it would be.

Train-versus-inference is, on a real GPU node, suspiciously easy. We don't claim the prototype proves much beyond "agent-on-logs is worth trying" — see the limits. The properties we'd want to tackle next are harder: FLOP-budget compliance, undeclared fine-tuning, "is the served model the declared model." Those are further down.

Laptop scale

Jason's half. We trained a 3.2-million-parameter character-level GPT — four transformer blocks, four heads, 256 hidden dimensions — on Tiny Shakespeare (a 1 MB corpus of the plays — yes, "something is rotten in the state of Denmark" is in there), on Apple Silicon's MPS backend (Apple's GPU compute backend, the M-series equivalent of CUDA).Spec4 layers · 4 heads · 256 hidden · seq 128 · vocab 65 · MPS · ~5 min wall clock for 9k steps. About five minutes of wall clock got us to the point where the model can tell ROMEO is followed by a colon. Then we ran inference on the trained checkpoint and captured another few minutes of token-by-token output.

Per training step, the log holds the things you'd put in a W&B dashboard, plus the things you'd want for an audit trail:

  • Loss; gradient norms (global and per parameter group); Adam optimiser state norms.
  • Phase timings (data load, forward, backward, optimiser); tokens per second; learning rate.
  • System telemetry: process RSS, MPS allocated and driver memory, CPU%, system load, network I/O.
  • Every fifty steps: full model fingerprint, top-five-largest-tensor min/max/mean/std summaries, and a per-parameter-group weight-norm snapshot.
  • A SHA-256 hash chained to the previous record. Reorder a record and the chain breaks. Edit one and the chain breaks.

Per inference token: the full softmax distribution at the moment of sampling, the attention pattern at every layer and every head, the KL divergence from the previous step's distribution at the same position, the latency, the KV-cache bytes. There is a thing for everything we might want to ask later.

The full capture is about 320 MB on disk: roughly 9,000 hash-chained training records, 240 inference records, 100 raw-tensor snapshots if you want the actual matrices, four full model checkpoints, and one Chrome profiler trace. Building the agent

The verifier itself is one Anthropic API call. System prompt frozen and cached for an hour; log payload cached for five minutes; structured JSON output. Default model is Claude Sonnet 4.6. The agent reads a trace, considers the schema, and emits a verdict — training or inference — with a stated confidence and a list of cited signals.

Across the seven canonical test cases — one clean training trace, one clean inference trace, and five adversarial mutations designed to fool a naive classifier — the agent returned the correct verdict in all seven, at roughly $0.15 per call.Most-cited signalsFLOP-per-token ratio (training is ~3× higher than inference) · presence/absence of a _real_training block · memory profile shape (training pre-allocates and stays flat; inference grows with the KV cache) · weight drift across the run. Per-case rationales are at /inspector/agent.

The red-team loop we ran alongside is more interesting than the verdicts. Claude versus Claude. Each round, a red-team agent looks at the schema, the existing five evasions, and the last ten rounds, and proposes a new attack: a function that mutates a clean log into a lying one that should still pass the verifier. The mutate function runs in a sandbox; the verifier classifies the result; the round is appended to a public JSONL.

We ran this for sixty rounds. Fifteen attacks succeeded — each one closed by a protocol patch the next round. Look at the patch list, not the verdict score. Every dimension of the schema needed an explicit uniqueness or stability constraint before the agent could be relied on (truncation, model-hash drift, op-type relabelling, hash-and-config rotation, token under-reporting, cross-job fingerprint pollution). By the end the protocol had grown from four implicit rules to nineteen. The full history is at /inspector/redteam.Patch trajectoryRules in protocol over the 60-round red-team loop. 4 → 19.

H100 testbed

Jasmine's half. On Apple Silicon you can read every memory counter you want; on a real datacenter you cannot. Cloud providers expose nvidia-smi at one-second granularity and call it a day. To get the telemetry an inspector would actually have to work with, we rented a pair of H100 SXM nodes on RunPod and instrumented them ourselves.Why this is hardMost of Jasmine's time went into wrestling cloud-provider abstractions to get telemetry that real labs already collect but don't share: DCGM at 10 Hz, NCCL logs, NIC counters. We think this is the most detailed public capture of GPU training telemetry that exists. Almost no one shares this granularity.

Each host runs the same eight-phase schedule — five honest phases, three adversarial ones. The honest phases cover a representative spread of real workloads:

  • 30-minute pretraining run on TinyStories with a small GPT.
  • 30-minute pretraining run with a larger GPT.
  • 15-minute inference run loading the prior checkpoint.
  • 20-minute fine-tune of the small model on WikiText.
  • 5-minute idle phase as a confound check.

The adversarial phases fight the labels:8-phase schedule5 honest phases (green) + 1 idle (neutral) + 3 adversarial (red), 145 min per host.

  • P1 · train-as-infer. A training phase declared as inference.
  • P2 · big-as-small. The larger model claimed to be the smaller one.
  • P3 · finetune-as-pretrain. A fine-tune declared as a fresh pretraining run.

Ground-truth labels live in workload_labels.jsonl alongside the prover's claimed labels. The whole capture is a label-flip benchmark: any verifier can be scored against it.By the numbers34,072 rows · 1.33 GB on disk · 2 hosts × 8 phases · captured under CC-BY-4.0 at huggingface.co/datasets/jasminexli/verifier-challenge-traces.

The same six rows of nvsmi tell quite different stories on a training and an inference phase. Training pegs at 100% utilisation and a memory plateau of ~75 GB; the GPU runs hot and steady at 412 W. Inference bounces between 50–90% utilisation with a power range of 140–310 W, while memory grows monotonically as the KV cache (the cached attention keys and values, which expand with each new token) builds up. Even from this single counter, sampled at 1 Hz, the difference is visible.

Per phase, on each host, six streams:

  • nvsmi.csv — nvidia-smi at 1 Hz: power draw, GPU utilisation, memory used, clocks, temperature. Standard CSV, the one number every cloud provider does expose.
  • dcgm.csv — DCGM (NVIDIA's data-center GPU manager) at 10 Hz: tensor-core utilisation, NVLink bandwidth, ECC errors, far more granular than nvidia-smi. Despite the name, this is space-separated text with #Entity headers, not a real CSV. (We learned this in the worst possible way.) Cloud providers do not expose DCGM by default.
  • nccl_<host>_<pid>.log.gz — NCCL (NVIDIA's collective-communications library) logs per rank, gzipped. Tells you the topology of communication: which ranks are sharing gradients, how often, over which links. Inference doesn't run NCCL collectives. Training does.
  • netdev.log.gz — /proc/net/dev at 1 Hz, gzipped. Cross-checks NCCL against actual NIC counters.
  • checkpoints/ — gpt_tiny.pt and gpt_small.pt. Real model state, hashable.
  • provenance.json — host metadata, GPU IDs, Python version, git commit, and a wall-clock skew estimate so the streams across the two hosts can be aligned.

The closest public peer datasets capture a single counter — usually nvidia-smi power — or collapse a whole phase into one summary row. People who run real workloads don't share this much; people who write about verification rarely run real workloads. The data isn't exotic. Almost no one collects it at this resolution.

What six streams give you that one stream doesn't:

  • Training writes to disk in distinctive bursts at the end of each step.
  • Inference's KV cache grows monotonically with context length.
  • NCCL collectives don't fire during inference.
  • Tensor-core utilisation drops in measurable ways when the model is smaller than declared.
  • Idle phases have a power floor around 80 W per GPU that nothing else has.

None of these are individually conclusive. Together they leave very little room for a prover to lie.

What's next

Train-versus-inference and model size are the two easy properties. The next ones are harder, and the ones any real verification regime will actually need.Properties × signalspropertyload-bearing signalFLOP cappower × time vs Kaplan floormodel sizetensor-core util, mem footprintundeclared finetuneNCCL bursts during "inference"served identityweight-checkpoint hash at loadWhat we'd want each verifier to read off first.

  • [medium] FLOP-budget compliance. Did this run consume more compute than the declared cap? FLOP, in case it's unfamiliar, is the unit of how much arithmetic the chip did — basically "compute spent." The Kaplan estimate (compute ≈ 6 × params × tokens, the standard scaling-law approximation) gives an independent floor; combined with hardware-side counters, the gap between declared and actual gets small.NoteTwo independent floors on declared compute: the Kaplan estimate (6 × params × tokens) and integrated power × time × FLOPs/W from hardware counters. Honest transformer runs agree within ~10%.
  • [medium] Model-size compliance. Stronger than the prototype's model-size check: not just "is the served model the declared one" but "is the trained model the one you said you were going to train."
  • [hard] Undeclared fine-tuning. A lab loads a checkpoint, runs a quiet fine-tune in the background, returns the result. This is the canonical hard case for a verifier with only datacenter access (the canonical defence: "we were not training, we were continuing the inference of the previous batch") and probably the most realistic abuse pattern.
  • [easy] Is the served model the declared model. Inference-side identity. Easier than training-side because you can fingerprint the weights at load time.

Other things on the list, roughly ordered by what we'd reach for first:

  • [easy] A Claude-written stripped-down verifier — the simplest possible program that does this reliably, assuming faithful logs. Useful as a baseline; useful as documentation.
  • [hard] A mechanistic write-up of which signals the agent is actually using to make its determination, head-by-head. Right now we know the cited signals; we do not have a reliable map from signal to weight.
  • [hard] An adversarial case the agent fails. We have fifteen patch-driven near-misses in the red-team loop; we do not yet have a clean failure mode that holds across protocol patches. Finding one is the test of the framework.NoteStandard log-based intrusion-detection evasion taxonomy applies: padding, substitution, fragmentation, encoding. The 60-round loop ported the first two.
  • [easy] A list of properties we cannot yet detect. Probably more useful to the field than another property we can.
  • [medium] Implementation on real production-shaped traces — we already have adapters for the Alibaba clusterdata v2023 trace and the BLOOM-176B chronicles. A clean published baseline would be the next step.
  • [medium] A first-pass adversary model — what an adversary actually does at scale. The cheap version: a single-prompt Claude red-teamer. The expensive version: a real engineering team running real evasions.
  • [hard] Realistic-subset verifier. Running on only the realistic subset of logs an inspector would see — input/output to the rack, with proprietary internal telemetry redacted. This is the version that matters for actual deployment, and the version we have not yet built.
  • [research] An open-source verifier model fine-tuned on the corpus and published on Hugging Face. Right now the verifier is a Sonnet wrapper. A self-contained 7B-class model would be the version a sovereign auditor could deploy on-prem.
  • [hard] Full 8×H100 implementation, including the cloud-provider workarounds. We expect this to be hard. Most of the difficulty in moving to frontier-scale verification lives there.NoteAWS doesn't expose DCGM. GCP partially does. RunPod, Lambda, and Crusoe give raw access. Verification testbeds need raw-access providers.
  • [research] A first-cut networking protocol for between-chip communication that an inspector could reasonably observe without reading inside any one chip.
  • [easy] A Fermi estimate of the hardware requirements to scale this approach to a production-scale datacenter — an honest TCO for the inspector itself.
  • [medium] Challenge prompts, leaderboard, evaluation harness — the mechanical surface that turns "an idea for a competition" into "a competition you can actually enter."

What we didn't do

  • Multi-node fabric. Single-host on the laptop, two hosts on the testbed. Real frontier training is hundreds of nodes with distinct topology. The hard part of verification is the fabric, not the chip.NoteCross-node verification needs per-rank NCCL + per-link bandwidth. NVLink is intra-node only; the inter-node fabric (InfiniBand, RoCE) is the harder verification target.
  • Hardware attestation. Nothing the prover writes into the log is cryptographically tied to the silicon that produced it. Without TEEs (Trusted Execution Environments — chips that can sign attestations of what they ran) or signed hardware attestations, a sufficiently sophisticated adversary can forge any log we read.NoteTEE-signed attestations exist on Intel SGX, AMD SEV-SNP, and (since 2024) H100 Confidential Computing. None used in this prototype.
  • Sovereign trust. The verifier is Claude calling out to Anthropic. No US–China deal will be enforced by an Anthropic API key. The path to a deployable inspector runs through an open-source narrow model that all parties can audit.
  • Compound attacks. Each red-team round mutates one dimension; we did not stress two attack classes at once.NoteThe realistic threat model is op_type relabel + token under-reporting + fingerprint rotation simultaneously. None of the 60 red-team rounds tested combinations.
  • n=2 hosts on the testbed. Real cross-host variance is unknown.
  • Train-vs-inference is suspiciously easy. The five-stream H100 telemetry separates training and inference roughly trivially. The harder properties (FLOP cap, undeclared fine-tune, identity) are the test of the framework.

Where this goes

We opened this post with three challenges. DARPA's Grand Challenge, in 2004, broke self-driving out of academic stasis and onto the streets of San Francisco inside a decade. ImageNet, in 2009, did the same for deep learning; modern AI is downstream of what AlexNet pulled off with two consumer GPUs in 2012. The Vesuvius Challenge, in 2023, recovered hundreds of scrolls that had been sealed for nineteen centuries.

The comparison isn't that AI verification is as hard as decoding a 79 AD scroll. It's that AI verification has the same shape: the problem is real, the solution path is unclear, the field is small enough that a public dataset and a leaderboard can move it materially, and almost nobody is funding it. Not because it isn't important. Because it falls between everyone's mandate.

AI is the most consequential technology of our time, and we still cannot verify the most basic facts about how it's built. That gap is the binding constraint on every form of AI governance worth wanting — labs proving they're keeping their own safety commitments, state regulators enforcing whatever rules eventually land, the US and China verifying any AI deal that gets signed. None of those work without a verifier all parties can see.

Show the phased plan
  • Phase 1, May–June (~$25k for compute, travel). Stand up a datacenter-scale MVP on a rented 8×H100 node — retrain GPT-2, capture the full multi-stream log, get the verifier reading real workloads. Begin collaborations with RAND, MIRI, DARPA, the State Department, the Frontier Model Forum, and the major cloud providers, to figure out which verification properties are actually load-bearing. Stretch goal: present the MVP at ISE 2026 in Singapore (a US–China AI governance exchange, May 17–19) and meet Chinese researchers in person.
  • Phase 2, June–September (~$70k for compute, talent). Lock the testbed for three-to-five challenge types — training-run attestation, training-vs-inference classification, undeclared-workload detection, FLOP-budget compliance, model-identity. Specify the scoring function and the adversarial budget. Send to frontier-lab security researchers to break before we publish.
  • Phase 3, October ($50k–$500k for prize, marketing, judging). Public Verifier Challenge launch. Open competition, prize pool, public leaderboard. The one we open the doors on.

If you have compute to share, frontier security expertise to pressure-test the testbed, AI-policy contacts who'd care about this, or a thesis on which property matters most — write to us. This is the smallest version of what we're trying to build. It runs on a laptop. The full thing won't.
